Math Recap

Math Recap: Bayes’ Rule

Suppose that \(A\) and \(B\) are stochastic events, then if \(P(B)>0\):

\[P(A|B)=\frac{P(A\cap B)}{P(B)}=\frac{P(B|A)P(A)}{P(B)}\]

Suppose that \(\Omega\) is the whole outcome space. Events \(A_{1},A_{2},\dots,A_{n}\) are called a partition if for any \(i\neq j\): \(A_{i}\cap A_{j}=\emptyset\) and \(\cup_{i=1}^{n}A_{i}=\Omega\). If \(P(B)>0\), then

\[P(A_{j}|B)=\frac{P(B|A_{j})P(A_{j})}{P(B)}=\frac{P(B|A_{j})P(A_{j})}{\sum_{i=1}^{n}P(B|A_{i})P(A_{i})}\]

Bayes’ Rule: Running Example

Two types of workers’ productivity: H (high) and L (low). Two types of job: W (white collar) and B (blue collar).

  • \(P(H)=\tfrac{1}{4}\), \(\quad P(W)=\tfrac{1}{3}\), \(\quad P(W|H)=\tfrac{5}{6}\)
  • A randomly chosen individual has a white collar job. What is \(P(H|W)\)?

\[P(H|W)=\frac{P(W|H)\,P(H)}{P(W)}=\frac{\tfrac{5}{6}\times\tfrac{1}{4}}{\tfrac{1}{3}}=\frac{5}{8}\]

Now suppose we do not know \(P(W)\), but instead have \(P(W|L)=\tfrac{1}{6}\). Using the law of total probability:

\[P(H|W)=\frac{P(W|H)\,P(H)}{P(W|H)\,P(H)+P(W|L)\,P(L)}=\frac{\tfrac{5}{6}\times\tfrac{1}{4}}{\tfrac{5}{6}\times\tfrac{1}{4}+\tfrac{1}{6}\times\tfrac{3}{4}}=\frac{5}{8}\]

Math Recap: Conditional Expectation (Discrete)

Suppose that a random variable \(X\) takes values \(x_{1},x_{2},\dots,x_{n}\) with probabilities \(P(X=x_{i})=p_{i}\) for any \(i=1,2,\dots,n\). Then, its expectation is

\[\mathbb{E}[X]=\sum_{i=1}^{n}x_{i}p_{i}\]

Now consider some condition \(A\) (e.g., \(X<10\)), then the conditional expectation given \(A\) is

\[\mathbb{E}[X|A]=\sum_{i=1}^{n}x_{i}\,P(X=x_{i}|A)\]

Discrete Expectation: Running Example (Setup)

Denote salary as \(w\) and productivity as \(\theta\). Three types of workers:

  • \(\theta_{H}=7\) with \(P(\theta=7)=0.25\): earns \(w\in\{7,8\}\) with equal probability
  • \(\theta_{M}=4\) with \(P(\theta=4)=0.5\): earns \(w\in\{5,6\}\) with equal probability
  • \(\theta_{L}=2\) with \(P(\theta=2)=0.25\): earns \(w\in\{2,3,4\}\) with equal probability

Each worker rejects any offer with salary lower than their productivity.

Question: A randomly chosen worker earns no more than $5 per hour. What is \(\mathbb{E}[\theta\mid w\leq 5]\)?

Discrete Expectation: Solution (Steps 1–2)

Step 1: High type workers always reject \(w\leq 5\) (since \(\theta_{H}=7>5\)), so \(P(\theta=7\mid w\leq 5)=0\).

Step 2: Find \(P(w\leq 5)\) by the law of total probability:

\[P(w\leq 5)=\underbrace{1}_{P(w\leq 5|\theta=2)}\times\tfrac{1}{4}+\underbrace{\tfrac{1}{2}}_{P(w\leq 5|\theta=4)}\times\tfrac{1}{2}+\underbrace{0}_{P(w\leq 5|\theta=7)}\times\tfrac{1}{4}=\frac{1}{2}\]

Discrete Expectation: Solution (Steps 3–4)

Step 3: Posterior probabilities (using Bayes’ rule):

\[P(\theta=4\mid w\leq 5)=\frac{P(w\leq 5|\theta=4)\,P(\theta=4)}{P(w\leq 5)}=\frac{\frac{1}{2}\times\frac{1}{2}}{\frac{1}{2}}=\frac{1}{2}\]

\[P(\theta=2\mid w\leq 5)=\frac{1\times\frac{1}{4}}{\frac{1}{2}}=\frac{1}{2}\]

Step 4: \(\mathbb{E}[\theta\mid w\leq 5]=2\times\tfrac{1}{2}+4\times\tfrac{1}{2}+7\times 0=3\)

Math Recap: Conditional Expectation (Continuous)

A continuous random variable \(X\) has pdf \(f(x)\) and cdf \(F(x)=P(X\leq x)=\int_{-\infty}^{x}f(t)\,dt\). Its expectation is \(\mathbb{E}[X]=\int x\,f(x)\,dx\).

The conditional expectation given an event \(A\) is

\[\mathbb{E}[X|A]=\int x\,f_{X|A}(x)\,dx=\frac{\int_{A}x\,f(x)\,dx}{\int_{A}f(x)\,dx}\]

Note that \(f_{X|A}(x)=\frac{f(x)}{P(X\in A)}\) if \(x\in A\). Be careful with the support of \(X\), because it gets restricted by \(A\).

Uniform Distribution

If \(X \mid a\leq X\leq b\) is distributed uniformly on \([c,d]\), then \(\mathbb{E}[X \mid a\leq X\leq b]=\frac{c+d}{2}\).

Continuous Expectation: Running Example

Suppose productivity \(\theta\) is distributed uniformly on \([1,5]\), i.e., \(f_{\theta}(x)=\tfrac{1}{4}\) if \(1\leq x\leq 5\).

Every worker earns salary according to their productivity: \(w=\theta\).

Question: A randomly chosen person earns less than $3. What is the expected productivity?

\[\mathbb{E}[\theta\mid w\leq 3]=\mathbb{E}[\theta\mid \theta\leq 3]=\mathbb{E}[\theta\mid 1\leq\theta\leq 3]=\frac{1+3}{2}=2\]

Note: restricting a uniform r.v. to a sub-interval gives another uniform r.v.

Adverse Selection

The Market for Lemons

A buyer wants to purchase a used car from a seller. The car is either high quality (a “peach”) or low quality (a “lemon”).

  • With probability \(\frac{1}{3}\) the car is a peach; with probability \(\frac{2}{3}\) it is a lemon
  • The seller values a peach at $2,000 and a lemon at $1,000
  • The buyer values a peach at $2,200 and a lemon at $1,200

First Best: Quality Known by Both

  • Peach: buyer values $2,200 > seller’s $2,000. Trade at any \(p\in[\$2{,}000, \$2{,}200]\)
  • Lemon: buyer values $1,200 > seller’s $1,000. Trade at any \(q\in[\$1{,}000, \$1{,}200]\)
  • All cars are sold. Gains from trade exist for both types.

Asymmetric Info: Price = $2,000

Now the seller knows the quality while the buyer does not.

Both types of sellers are willing to sell at $2,000. The expected value for the buyer is:

\[\mathbb{E}[v]=\tfrac{1}{3}\times 2{,}200+\tfrac{2}{3}\times 1{,}200=1{,}533.33\]

Buyer’s expected payoff: \(1{,}533.33-2{,}000=-466.67\)

The buyer loses money on average – will not buy at this price!

No cars are sold.

Asymmetric Info: Price = $1,533.33

What kind of car would the seller agree to sell at this price?

  • Peach seller: value $2,000 > $1,533.33 – will not sell
  • Lemon seller: value $1,000 < $1,533.33 – will sell

Only lemons are offered! The buyer’s expected value:

\[\mathbb{E}[v\mid v\leq 1{,}200]=\$1{,}200\]

Buyer’s expected payoff: \(1{,}200-1{,}533.33=-333.33\)

The buyer will not buy. No cars are sold.

Asymmetric Info: Price = $1,150

Again, only lemon sellers participate (peach value $2,000 > $1,150).

The buyer knows this: \(\mathbb{E}[v]=\$1{,}200\)

Buyer’s expected payoff: \(1{,}200-1{,}150=50>0\)

The buyer will purchase. But only lemons are sold!

Key Result: Market for Lemons

Asymmetric information causes adverse selection: high-quality cars are driven out of the market. The result is either partial market failure (only lemons trade) or complete market failure (no trade at all).

Adverse Selection

Definition: Adverse Selection

Adverse selection is a phenomenon when a better-informed party selectively participates in trade by often offering only a low-quality product.

Similar partial or complete market failure arises in:

  • Investments into start-ups
  • Insurance markets
  • Labor markets

Takeover Game

  • The buyer can improve the value of an existing company by 50%
  • \(v\) is the exact value of the company, known only to the seller (seller’s type)
    • \(v\) is distributed uniformly on \((0, 100)\)
  • If the seller of type \(v\) accepts an offer of price \(p\) to sell the company, then the buyer receives \(1.5v - p\), whereas the seller gets \(p\)
  • If the seller rejects the offer, the buyer receives \(0\) and the seller retains the company at its current value \(v\)

Takeover Game: First Best

Suppose the buyer knows the value \(v\). Will the trade happen?

The buyer values the company at \(1.5v\), the seller at \(v\). Since \(1.5v>v\), the gains from trade are \(0.5v>0\).

Any price \(p\) such that \(v\leq p\leq 1.5v\) makes both parties willing to trade.

First Best

When the buyer knows \(v\), trade always occurs. Both parties benefit.

Takeover Game: Asymmetric Information

The buyer does not know \(v\). On average: \(\mathbb{E}[v]=50\) and \(\mathbb{E}[1.5v]=75\). Looks profitable!

But the seller accepts only if \(v\leq p\). A seller with \(v=70\) will reject \(p=60\).

The buyer should expect the value of the company to be no higher than \(p\):

\[\mathbb{E}[1.5v\mid v\leq p]=1.5\,\mathbb{E}[v\mid 0\leq v\leq p]=1.5\times\frac{p}{2}=0.75p\]

Since \(0.75p<p\) for all \(p>0\), the buyer always loses money!

Key Result

Trade is impossible despite gains from trade. The buyer’s expected value (\(0.75p\)) is always below the price (\(p\)).

Takeover Game: When Trade Survives

What if the buyer values the company at \(3v\) instead of \(1.5v\)?

\[\mathbb{E}[3v\mid v\leq p]=3\times\frac{p}{2}=1.5p>p\]

The buyer’s expected value exceeds the price – trade occurs!

Takeaway

Adverse selection does not always destroy the market. If the gains from trade are sufficiently large, the market might still result in an efficient outcome despite asymmetric information.

Labor Market

  • Each worker has productivity \(\theta\in[0,1]\), which is private information
  • All workers have an outside option (reservation wage) \(r(\theta)=0.75\theta\)
  • The labor market is competitive, so the firms receive zero profit

Productivity is observed by both parties:

  • Firm’s profit: \(\pi=\theta-w\)
  • Competition \(\Rightarrow\) \(w=\theta\)
  • The worker accepts: \(w=\theta\geq r(\theta)=0.75\theta\)
  • Thus, it is efficient for any type of the worker to be hired

Labor Market: Asymmetric Information

The firm does not observe \(\theta\) and believes \(\theta\sim U[0,1]\).

When the firm offers wage \(w\), only workers with \(0.75\theta\leq w\) accept (i.e., \(\theta\leq\tfrac{4}{3}w\)).

The expected productivity of a worker who accepts:

\[\mathbb{E}\!\left[\theta\mid 0.75\theta\leq w\right]=\mathbb{E}\!\left[\theta\mid 0\leq\theta\leq\tfrac{4}{3}w\right]=\frac{0+\frac{4}{3}w}{2}=\frac{2}{3}w\]

Firm’s expected profit:

\[\mathbb{E}[\pi\mid 0.75\theta\leq w]=\frac{2}{3}w-w=-\frac{1}{3}w<0\]

Key Result: Complete Market Failure

No wage can make hiring profitable. The expected productivity of workers who accept (\(\frac{2}{3}w\)) is always below the wage (\(w\)). The labor market collapses entirely.

Adverse Selection: Summary

The same pattern appears in all three examples:

First Best Asymmetric Info
Lemons All cars sold Only lemons trade (or no trade)
Takeover Trade always occurs \(0.75p < p\): no trade
Labor All workers hired \(\frac{2}{3}w < w\): no hiring

The Adverse Selection Tradeoff

Two parties can conduct a trade that would be beneficial to both – if only they can agree on a price. But asymmetric information causes the informed party to select adversely, making the uninformed party’s expected payoff negative and potentially destroying the market.

Signaling

Signaling as a Cure

Consider the lemons market again. Suppose the buyer can take the car to a mechanic who can distinguish a lemon with probability 90%.

  • \(P(\text{declared lemon} \mid \text{lemon})=0.9\)
  • \(P(\text{declared peach} \mid \text{lemon})=0.1\)
  • \(P(\text{declared peach} \mid \text{peach})=1\)

Suppose the mechanic declares the car a peach and the seller asks \(p\geq\$2{,}000\).

What is the expected value of the car to the buyer?

Signaling: Bayes Calculation

We need \(P(\text{peach}\mid\text{declared peach})\):

\[P(\text{peach}\mid\text{declared peach})=\frac{P(\text{declared peach}\mid\text{peach})\,P(\text{peach})}{P(\text{declared peach})}\]

Using the law of total probability for the denominator:

\[P(\text{declared peach})=\underbrace{1}_{P(\text{d.p.}\mid\text{peach})}\times\tfrac{1}{3}+\underbrace{0.1}_{P(\text{d.p.}\mid\text{lemon})}\times\tfrac{2}{3}=\frac{1}{3}+\frac{2}{30}=\frac{12}{30}=\frac{2}{5}\]

\[P(\text{peach}\mid\text{declared peach})=\frac{1\times\frac{1}{3}}{\frac{2}{5}}=\frac{5}{6}\]

\[P(\text{lemon}\mid\text{declared peach})=1-\frac{5}{6}=\frac{1}{6}\]

Signaling: Trade Restored

The expected value of a car declared as a peach:

\[\mathbb{E}[v\mid\text{declared peach}]=\frac{5}{6}\times 2{,}200+\frac{1}{6}\times 1{,}200\approx 2{,}033\]

Since \(\mathbb{E}[v]=2{,}033>\$2{,}000\), trade is now possible for any price \(\$2{,}000\leq p\leq \$2{,}033\).

Key Result: Signaling Restores Trade

The mechanic’s signal raises \(P(\text{peach}\mid\text{declared peach})\) from \(\frac{1}{3}\) to \(\frac{5}{6}\), making trade possible. Signaling can cure adverse selection by reducing information asymmetry.

Moral Hazard

Moral Hazard: Hidden Action

Principal–Agent problem:

  • A principal is an owner of a firm
  • An agent is a manager
  • The profit of the firm depends on effort of the manager \(e\) and a random component (economic conditions) \(\varepsilon\): \(\pi = e + \varepsilon\)
    • The owner observes the profit, but does not know effort \(e\)
    • The only information about \(\varepsilon\) for the owner: \(\mathbb{E}[\varepsilon]=0\)
  • The owner maximizes: \(u_{p}(\pi, w) = \mathbb{E}[\pi - w(\pi)]\), where \(w(\pi)\) is wage paid to the manager
  • The manager maximizes: \(u_{a}(w, e) = \mathbb{E}[w(\pi)] - c(e)\), where \(c(e)\) is the cost of putting effort \(e\)
    • Suppose \(c(e) = e^{2}\)

Key Question

How should the owner choose the wage scheme \(w(\pi)\) in order to maximize their own utility?

Constant Wage

Suppose the wage is constant: \(w(\pi)=w\) for any \(\pi\).

The agent’s utility: \(u_{a}=\mathbb{E}[w(\pi)]-e^{2}=w-e^{2}\)

  • Does not depend on \(\pi\), and negatively depends on effort \(e\)
  • The manager will choose zero effort: \(e=0\)

With zero effort: \(\mathbb{E}[\pi]=\mathbb{E}[e+\varepsilon]=0\)

Owner’s utility: \(u_{p}=\mathbb{E}[\pi]-w=0-w=-w<0\)

Key Insight

A constant wage provides zero incentive for effort. The manager shirks completely, and the owner does not want to hire at all.

First Best (Observable Effort)

Suppose the effort is observable by the principal. The owner solves:

\[\max_{w,e}\;\mathbb{E}[\pi]-w=e-w \qquad\text{s.t.}\quad w-e^{2}\geq 0 \;\text{(IR constraint)}\]

The IR constraint ensures the agent is willing to work. With market power, the principal sets it to bind: \(w=e^{2}\).

\[\max_{e}\;e-e^{2}\quad\Rightarrow\quad 1-2e=0\quad\Rightarrow\quad e^{*}=0.5,\quad w^{*}=0.25\]

The wage scheme: \(w(e)=\begin{cases}0.25&\text{if }e=0.5\\0&\text{otherwise}\end{cases}\)

First Best

With observable effort: \(e^{*}=0.5\), \(w^{*}=0.25\). Owner’s payoff \(=0.5-0.25=0.25\).

Second Best: Linear Contract

Now effort is unobservable. The wage must depend on the observable outcome (profit): \(w(\pi)=a+b\pi\).

The agent’s utility:

\[u_{a}=\mathbb{E}[w(\pi)]-e^{2}=a+b\,\mathbb{E}[\pi]-e^{2}=a+be-e^{2}\]

Agent’s optimal effort (incentive compatibility):

\[\text{FOC:}\quad b-2e=0\quad\Rightarrow\quad e=\frac{b}{2}\]

Equilibrium wage: \(\mathbb{E}[w]=a+b\cdot\frac{b}{2}=a+\frac{b^{2}}{2}\)

IR constraint: \(u_{a}=a+\frac{b^{2}}{4}\geq 0\)

Second Best: Optimal Contract

The principal’s problem (IR binds \(\Rightarrow\) \(a=-\tfrac{b^{2}}{4}\)):

\[\max_{b}\;\mathbb{E}[\pi]-\mathbb{E}[w]=\frac{b}{2}-a-\frac{b^{2}}{2}=\frac{b}{2}-\frac{b^{2}}{4}\]

\[\text{FOC:}\quad \frac{1}{2}-\frac{b}{2}=0\quad\Rightarrow\quad b^{*}=1,\quad a^{*}=-0.25\]

The optimal linear contract:

\[w(\pi)=-0.25+\pi\]

Key Result: Sell the Firm!

The optimal contract gives the manager the entire profit minus a fixed fee of $0.25. To elicit the best effort, the owner must effectively sell the firm to the manager!

Summary

Week 13: Key Takeaways

  1. Adverse selection arises when a better-informed party selectively participates in trade, offering low-quality goods
  2. In the Market for Lemons, only lemons trade (or no trade occurs) – the uninformed buyer’s expected value falls below the price
  3. The Takeover Game and Labor Market confirm the same pattern: \(\mathbb{E}[\text{value}\mid\text{accept}]<\text{price}\)
  4. Signaling (e.g., mechanic inspection) can restore trade by updating beliefs via Bayes’ rule
  5. Moral hazard arises from hidden actions; constant wages induce zero effort
  6. The optimal solution to moral hazard is to tie compensation to profit – effectively “sell the firm” to the manager
