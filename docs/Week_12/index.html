Part 1: Dynamic Games

Entry into an Industry

A monopolist (I) holds a patent. After the patent expires, an entrant (E) considers entering. If E enters, I could start a price war.

Entry: Normal Form

Strategy sets: \(S_E = \{Out, In\}\), \(S_I = \{Accommodate, Fight\}\). Best responses are underlined.

The normal form representation of the entry game. Two NE: (Out, Fight) and (In, Accommodate).

Entry: Two Nash Equilibria

There are two NE: (Out, Fight) and (In, Accommodate).

However, the equilibrium (Out, Fight) does not look plausible:

  • After E enters, I has no incentive to follow through on its plan to fight
  • Fighting gives I a payoff of \(-1\), while accommodating gives \(1\)
  • The threat to fight is not credible

Key Insight: NE Refinement

We would like to impose conditions to guarantee that players anticipate sensible things “off the equilibrium path.”

Proper Subgame

Denote: \(t\) — a node in the game; \(h(t)\) — the information set containing \(t\); \(S(t)\) — the set of all successors to the node \(t\).

Definition: Proper Subgame

A proper subgame of an extensive form game is \(\{t\}\cup S(t)\) such that \(h(t)=\{t\}\) (\(t\) is a unique node in its information set) and \(\forall t'\in S(t), h(t')\subseteq S(t)\) (successors are included together with their information sets).

Subgame Perfect Nash Equilibrium

Definition: Subgame Perfect Nash Equilibrium

A NE profile is subgame perfect if and only if the restriction of this profile to every proper subgame is a NE in that subgame.

Backward Induction

For finite games, one can solve for subgame perfect Nash equilibria (SPNE) by using backward induction on the subgames of the extensive form:

  1. At every last decision node, determine the optimal action.
  2. At every penultimate decision node, determine the optimal action, assuming that the actions determined in step 1 will be taken at the last node.
  3. \(\dots\)

Theorem: Backward Induction

Every finite perfect information game has a backward induction solution. Moreover, if no two payoffs are the same for any player, then there is a unique backward induction solution.

Part 2: Applications of Backward Induction

Stackelberg Model

A good example of a dynamic game with complete information and strategies in a continuum space is the Stackelberg model of sequential quantity competition.

  • The market demand is \(P = 100 - Q\)
  • Two identical firms with cost function \(c(q) = 4q\)
  • Firm 1 (leader) moves first and chooses its quantity \(q_1\)
  • Firm 2 (follower) observes the choice of firm 1 and then makes its production decision \(q_2\)

Stackelberg: Firm 2’s Best Response

Backward induction suggests to start with firm 2 and derive its best response to \(q_1\):

\[\pi_2 = (100 - q_1 - q_2)q_2 - 4q_2 \to \max_{q_2}\]

FOC:

\[100 - q_1 - 2q_2 - 4 = 0 \Rightarrow q_2 = \frac{96 - q_1}{2}\]

This is firm 2’s best response function. It tells us the optimal quantity for firm 2 as a function of firm 1’s quantity.

Stackelberg: Firm 1’s Decision

Firm 1 moves first and knows what best response \(q_2\) will be. It takes this into account:

\[\pi_1 = \left(100 - q_1 - \frac{96 - q_1}{2}\right)q_1 - 4q_1 = \left(\frac{96 - q_1}{2}\right)q_1 \to \max_{q_1}\]

FOC:

\[0.5(96 - 2q_1) = 0 \Rightarrow q_1 = 48\]

\[q_2 = \frac{96 - 48}{2} = 24\]

Stackelberg: Reaction Functions

The dotted curves are Firm 1’s iso-profit curves: all (q1, q2) combinations giving the same profit. Lower curves = higher profit (less competition). Firm 1 picks the point on BR2 that reaches the lowest iso-profit curve — the tangency point. This is analogous to utility maximization subject to a budget constraint: here Firm 1 maximizes profit subject to the constraint that Firm 2 plays its best response.

Stackelberg: SPNE

SPNE

The unique SPNE of this game is \(\left(q_1 = 48,\; q_2 = \frac{96 - q_1}{2}\right)\).

Equilibrium quantities: \(q_1^* = 48\), \(q_2^* = 24\).

Are there other NE that are not SPNE? Yes.

In order to find a NE that is not SPNE, firm 2 has to behave irrationally “off the equilibrium path.” There is an infinite number of such NE.

One example: \(q_1 = 16\) and \(q_2 = \begin{cases} 40, & \text{if } q_1 = 16 \\ 100, & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}\)

Firm 2 threatens to flood the market (q2=100) if firm 1 deviates. This is not credible because firm 2 would never actually produce 100 units.

Ultimatum Game

Player 1 conditionally receives $100 and proposes how to divide the sum between her and Player 2. Player 2 chooses to either accept or reject the proposal.

  • If Player 2 accepts, the money is split according to the proposal
  • If Player 2 rejects, neither player receives any money

What is SPNE?

Ultimatum Game: SPNE

SPNE

The SPNE is \((x = 0, \text{ accept if } x \geq 0)\).

The ultimatum game is a classic example where experimental results diverge sharply from the SPNE prediction. In experiments, offers below 20-30% are frequently rejected.

Bank Runs

Two investors have each deposited 2k with a bank. The bank has invested these deposits in a long-term project.

  • If the bank is forced to liquidate early (date 1), a total of 2k can be recovered
  • If the investment reaches maturity (date 2), the project pays out 6k total
  • There are two dates at which investors can withdraw

Bank Runs: Game Tree

Date 1: both investors simultaneously choose W or D. If both choose D, the game continues to date 2 where they again simultaneously choose W or D. Dashed lines indicate information sets (simultaneous moves).

Bank Runs: Backward Induction

Step 1: At date 2, W is a strictly dominant strategy for both investors. SPNE payoffs from date 2: \((3, 3)\).

Step 2: By backward induction, replacing date 2 with \((3, 3)\), date 1 reduces to:

Three NE at date 1: (W,W), (D,D), and a mixed strategy equilibrium.

Bank Runs: Three SPNE

The initial game has three SPNE:

  1. \(((W|1, W|2),\; (W|1, W|2))\) — both withdraw at both dates
  2. \(((D|1, W|2),\; (D|1, W|2))\) — both wait, then withdraw at date 2
  3. A mixed strategy equilibrium

Key Insight: Bank Runs

If one investor believes the other will withdraw at date 1, then it is a best response to withdraw as well — even though both investors would be better off waiting until date 2. This is a bank run.

Part 3: Repeated Games

Repeated Games

How to sustain cooperation when it is a dominated strategy? Think about the Prisoner’s Dilemma: in real life, we do not always observe “Defect.” Why?

In many strategic situations, players interact repeatedly over time. The promise of future rewards or the threat of future punishments may provide the incentives for cooperative behavior today.

Definition: Repeated Game

A repeated game is a special kind of game (in extensive or strategic form) where the same one-shot “stage” game is played over and over again.

Time Discounting

We assume that future payoffs are discounted: money in the future is less valuable than money now (positive interest rates, time preference).

Discount factor \(\beta \in [0, 1)\)

Example: If period 1 payoff is \(5\) and period 2 payoff is \(3\), then the present value at period 1 is \(5 + 3\beta\).

\(T\) periods with payoffs \(u_t\) for all \(t \leq T\): the present value at period 0 is

\[V_T = \sum_{t=0}^{T} \beta^t u_t\]

If all payoffs are the same (\(u_t = u\)): \(\quad V_T = \frac{1 - \beta^{T+1}}{1 - \beta} u\)

If \(T\) is infinite: \(\quad V = \frac{u}{1 - \beta}\)

Finitely Repeated Prisoner’s Dilemma

Consider a finitely repeated Prisoner’s Dilemma played for \(T\) rounds.

By backward induction: in round \(T\), both players Defect (dominant strategy). In round \(T-1\), knowing both will Defect in \(T\), both Defect again. And so on…

Key Result

The unique SPNE of a finitely repeated Prisoner’s Dilemma is to always Defect in every round.

Infinitely Repeated Games

Some predictions of finitely repeated games do not hold in reality. Moreover, in real life, we do not usually know when the game ends.

Now consider an infinitely repeated Prisoner’s Dilemma. The game has no end, so we cannot use backward induction.

  • “On equilibrium path” – the subgames that are reached in the equilibrium
  • “Off equilibrium path” – the subgames that are never actually played

Grim-Trigger Strategy

Definition: Grim-Trigger Strategy

Play Cooperate until someone deviates, then play Defect forever.

To check whether grim-trigger constitutes a SPNE, we use the one-shot deviation principle.

One-Shot Deviation Principle

Theorem: One-Shot Deviation Principle

A strategy profile \((s_1^*, s_2^*)\) is a SPNE if in every subgame no player can increase her payoffs by changing just her first action, while keeping all of her other actions (as well as her opponent’s strategy) fixed.

Thus, we need to check only two deviations: on-path (from C) and off-path (from D).

Grim-Trigger: On-Path Analysis

Consider player 1. Does she want to deviate from C?

Payoff from cooperation (play C forever): \(u(s_1^*, s_2^*) = (-1) + (-1)\beta + (-1)\beta^2 + \cdots = \frac{-1}{1 - \beta}\)

Payoff from deviating (play D once, then stuck with (D,D) forever): \(u(s_1', s_2^*) = 0 + (-7)\beta + (-7)\beta^2 + \cdots = \frac{-7\beta}{1 - \beta}\)

She will not deviate if and only if:

\[\frac{-1}{1 - \beta} \geq \frac{-7\beta}{1 - \beta} \iff \beta \geq \frac{1}{7}\]

Grim-Trigger: Off-Path Analysis

Does player 1 want to deviate from D if a deviation has already happened?

Payoff from Defect (play D forever): \(u(s_1^*, s_2^*) = (-7) + (-7)\beta + (-7)\beta^2 + \cdots = \frac{-7}{1 - \beta}\)

Payoff from deviating (play C once, then back to (D,D)): \(u(s_1', s_2^*) = -10 + (-7)\beta + (-7)\beta^2 + \cdots = -3 + \frac{-7}{1 - \beta}\)

Since \(\frac{-7}{1-\beta} \geq -3 + \frac{-7}{1-\beta}\) always holds (i.e., \(0 \geq -3\)), it is never profitable to deviate “off the path” for any \(\beta\).

Grim-Trigger: Cooperation Threshold

Grim-Trigger: Conclusion

Key Result: Grim-Trigger as SPNE

The grim-trigger strategy constitutes a SPNE as long as players are patient enough and value the future payoffs, i.e., \(\beta \geq \frac{1}{7}\).

Intuition:

  • The short-run gain from deviating: get \(0\) instead of \(-1\) (gain of \(1\))
  • The long-run cost of punishment: get \(-7\) instead of \(-1\) forever (loss of \(6\) per period)
  • When \(\beta\) is large enough, the future cost outweighs the short-run gain

Summary

Week 12: Key Takeaways

  1. Dynamic games involve sequential moves; the extensive form (game tree) is the natural representation

  2. Subgame perfection refines Nash equilibrium by requiring optimality in every subgame — eliminates non-credible threats

  3. Backward induction is the standard solution method for finite perfect information games

  4. Stackelberg competition illustrates first-mover advantage (\(q_1^* = 48 > q_2^* = 24\))

  5. Bank runs arise when rational behavior leads to a coordination failure — a Pareto inferior equilibrium

  6. Repeated games allow for cooperation through strategies like grim-trigger; the discount factor \(\beta\) determines whether cooperation is sustainable (\(\beta \geq \frac{1}{7}\))
