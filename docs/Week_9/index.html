Short-Run Costs

Motivation — Short Run vs Long Run

  • Recall from Week 8: in the long run, the firm can adjust all inputs optimally.

  • In the short run, some inputs (e.g., capital \(K\)) are fixed — machinery, factory size, land cannot be adjusted quickly.

  • Key question: How does fixing an input change the firm’s cost structure and behaviour?

The Short-Run Cost Minimisation Problem

Capital is fixed at \(\bar{K}\). The firm chooses only labour \(L\).

\[\min_{L} \; r\bar{K} + wL \quad \text{s.t.} \; F(\bar{K}, L) = q\]

The constraint pins down \(L\) as a function of \(q\) and \(\bar{K}\): the short-run conditional demand for labour \(L^c(q \mid \bar{K})\).

Short-run cost function:

\[c_{SR}(q) = \underbrace{r\bar{K}}_{\text{fixed cost}} + \underbrace{w \, L^c(q \mid \bar{K})}_{\text{variable cost}}\]

Worked Example — Cobb-Douglas SR Cost

\(F(K, L) = K^{1/3} L^{2/3}\), \(r = 2\), \(w = 4\), \(\bar{K} = 100\).

From the constraint: \(\bar{K}^{1/3} L^{2/3} = q \;\Rightarrow\; L^{2/3} = q \, \bar{K}^{-1/3}\)

\[L = q^{3/2} \bar{K}^{-1/2} = q^{3/2} / 10 = 0.1\, q^{3/2}\]

\[c_{SR}(q) = r\bar{K} + wL = 200 + 0.4\, q^{3/2}\]

Compare with long-run cost: \(c(q) = 6q\) (linear in \(q\)) — a very different shape!

Types of Costs

Types of Costs — Definitions

  • Total cost (TC): \(c(q) = FC + VC(q)\)

  • Fixed cost (FC): costs associated with fixed inputs; does not depend on \(q\). (\(\frac{\partial FC}{\partial q} = 0\))

  • Variable cost (VC): costs associated with variable inputs; depends on \(q\). (\(\frac{\partial VC}{\partial q} > 0\))

In our Cobb-Douglas example: \(FC = r\bar{K} = 200\), \(\; VC(q) = 0.4\, q^{3/2}\).

Average Costs — Definitions

  • Average cost (AC): \(AC = \frac{TC}{q}\) — cost per unit of output.

  • Average fixed cost (AFC): \(AFC = \frac{FC}{q}\) — decreasing in \(q\) (spreading fixed costs).

  • Average variable cost (AVC): \(AVC = \frac{VC}{q}\).

  • Identity: \(AC = AFC + AVC\).

Marginal Cost — Definition and Relationship to \(MP_L\)

Marginal cost (MC): \(MC = \frac{\partial TC}{\partial q} = \frac{\partial VC}{\partial q}\).

Since \(c(q) = wL(q) + r\bar{K}\):

\[MC = w \cdot \frac{dL}{dq} = \frac{w}{MP_L}\]

Key insight: MC is inversely related to the marginal product of labour.

  • More productive labour → lower MC
  • Diminishing \(MP_L\) → increasing MC
  • This connects production theory (Week 8) directly to cost theory (Week 9)

Cost Curve Relationships

The GPA Analogy — MC “Pulls” AC

Think of AC as your cumulative GPA, and MC as the grade in this class.

  • If your grade this semester (MC) is above your GPA (AC), your GPA rises.
  • If your grade this semester (MC) is below your GPA (AC), your GPA falls.

Formally:

\[AC'(q) = \frac{MC - AC}{q}\]

So AC is increasing when \(MC > AC\) and decreasing when \(MC < AC\).

Key Relationships — MC Intersects AC and AVC at Their Minima

Rule 1: As \(q \to \infty\), \(AC \to AVC\) (since \(AFC \to 0\)).

Rule 2: MC intersects AVC and AC at their respective minima.

Proof for AVC: \(\min_q AVC = \min_q \frac{VC}{q}\). FOC: \(\frac{MC \cdot q - VC}{q^2} = 0 \;\Rightarrow\; MC = \frac{VC}{q} = AVC\). Same logic applies to AC.

Rule 3: If MC is increasing, then \(q_{\min AVC} < q_{\min AC}\).

Graphical interpretation: MC is the slope of the TC curve; AC is the slope of a ray from the origin. MC = AC when the ray is tangent to TC.

U-Shaped Average Cost and Returns to Scale

Why is AC typically U-shaped?

  • Low \(q\): technology exhibits IRS → AC decreasing
  • Medium \(q\): technology exhibits CRS → AC roughly constant
  • High \(q\): technology exhibits DRS → AC increasing

Formal connection:

  • DRS ⇒ doubling inputs less than doubles output ⇒ AC increasing
  • CRS ⇒ doubling inputs exactly doubles output ⇒ AC constant, \(c(q)\) linear
  • IRS ⇒ doubling inputs more than doubles output ⇒ AC decreasing

Short-Run vs Long-Run Cost Examples

SR vs LR Costs — Leontief Technology

\(F(K, L) = (\min(K, 2L))^2\), \(\; r = 2\), \(w = 1\), \(\bar{K} = 10\).

Long run: At corner of isoquant, \(K = 2L\). Then \(q = K^2\), so \(K = \sqrt{q}\), \(L = \frac{\sqrt{q}}{2}\).

\[c(q) = 2\sqrt{q} + 0.5\sqrt{q} = 2.5\sqrt{q}\]

IRS technology; \(AC = 2.5\, q^{-1/2}\) is decreasing.

Short run: \(\bar{K} = 10\), so \(q \leq \bar{K}^2 = 100\) (capacity constraint).

For \(q \leq 100\): \(L = \frac{\sqrt{q}}{2}\); \(\; c_{SR}(q) = 20 + 0.5\sqrt{q}\).

SR vs LR Costs — Perfect Substitutes Technology

\(F(K, L) = (K + 2L)^{1/2}\), \(\; r = 2\), \(w = 3\), \(\bar{K} = 10\).

Long run: Compare cost-effectiveness: \(\frac{1}{r} = \frac{1}{2}\) vs \(\frac{2}{w} = \frac{2}{3}\). Since \(\frac{1}{r} < \frac{2}{w}\), labour is more productive per dollar → use only labour. \(2L = q^2\), so \(L = q^2/2\).

\[c(q) = 1.5\, q^2 \qquad \text{DRS technology; } AC = 1.5q \text{ is increasing.}\]

Short run: If \(q \leq \sqrt{\bar{K}} = \sqrt{10}\): capital alone suffices, \(c_{SR}(q) = r\bar{K} = 20\).

If \(q > \sqrt{10}\): need labour, \(L = \frac{q^2 - \bar{K}}{2}\). \(\; c_{SR}(q) = 5 + 1.5\, q^2\).

Piecewise cost function with kink at \(q = \sqrt{10}\).

Profit Maximisation and Supply

Profit Maximisation for a Competitive Firm

A competitive (price-taking) firm faces market price \(p\). Revenue \(= p \times q\).

\[\pi(q) = pq - c(q)\]

Marginal profit: \(\pi'(q) = p - MC(q)\).

Setting \(\pi'(q) = 0\): the firm produces where \(\boxed{p = MC}\).

Graphically: profit \(= (p - AC) \times q\).

Interactive: Profit Maximisation

Drag the output slider to see how revenue, cost, and profit respond. The firm maximises profit where \(p = MC\).

Short-Run Shut-Down Decision — Case 1: \(p > AVC\)

Even if profit is negative (\(p < AC\)), the firm may still choose to operate.

If \(p > AVC\): Revenue covers all variable costs and part of fixed costs.

  • Revenue \(= p \times q\)
  • Loss if operating \(= (AC - p) \times q\)
  • Loss if shut down \(= FC = (AC - AVC) \times q\)

Since \(p > AVC\): operating loss < FC. Operating is better — the firm loses less than its fixed costs.

Short-Run Shut-Down Decision — Case 2: \(p < AVC\)

If \(p < AVC\): Revenue does not even cover variable costs.

  • Loss if operating \(= FC + (AVC - p) \times q > FC\)
  • Loss if shut down \(= FC\)

Shutting down is better — the firm would lose more than its fixed costs by operating.

Conclusion: The firm shuts down if and only if \(p < AVC\) at the profit-maximising quantity.

The Shut-Down Price

The shut-down price is the borderline: \(p_{\text{shut}} = \min AVC = MC\) at \(q_{\min AVC}\).

Worked example: \(c(q) = 5 + 14q - 5q^2 + q^3\).

  • \(AVC = 14 - 5q + q^2\). FOC: \(-5 + 2q = 0 \;\Rightarrow\; q = 2.5\).
  • \(p_{\text{shut}} = 14 - 5(2.5) + 2.5^2 = 7.75\).

Verify: \(MC = 14 - 10q + 3q^2\); at \(q = 2.5\): \(MC = 14 - 25 + 18.75 = 7.75\). ✓

Short-Run Supply Curve

The firm’s SR supply curve:

\[q^*(p) = \begin{cases} 0 & \text{if } p < p_{\text{shut}} \\ MC^{-1}(p) & \text{if } p \geq p_{\text{shut}} \end{cases}\]

Graphically: the SR supply curve is the portion of the MC curve above \(\min AVC\).

Interactive: Supply and Cost Curves

Long-Run Shut-Down Price

In the long run, all costs are variable (no fixed costs to “sink”).

Long-run shut-down price: \(p_{LR} = \min AC = MC\) at \(q_{\min AC}\).

If \(p < p_{LR}\), the firm exits the market entirely (long-run supply = 0).

Partial Equilibrium

Partial Equilibrium — Setup

Partial equilibrium analyses supply and demand in one market only, ignoring other markets.

Assumptions of perfect competition:

  1. Many buyers and sellers (no market power)
  2. Homogeneous good
  3. Perfect information
  4. Free entry and exit (important for long run)

Equilibrium price \(p^*\): the price at which \(Q^d(p^*) = Q^s(p^*)\).

From Firm Supply to Market Supply

Each firm \(j\) has supply \(s^j(p)\) (from \(p = MC\)).

\[S(p) = \sum_{j=1}^{N_F} s^j(p)\]

With \(N_F\) identical firms: \(S(p) = N_F \cdot s(p)\).

Graphically: horizontal summation of individual supply curves.

Interactive: Market Supply with Identical Firms

Short-Run Equilibrium — Worked Example

\(Q^d = 100 - p\); \(\;\) 10 firms with \(c(q) = 5 + 14q + 5q^2\).

  • Individual supply: \(p = MC = 14 + 10q \;\Rightarrow\; q^S = 0.1(p - 14)\)
  • Aggregate supply: \(Q^s = 10\, q^S = p - 14\)
  • Equilibrium: \(100 - p = p - 14 \;\Rightarrow\; p^* = 57\), \(Q^* = 43\)
  • Each firm: \(q^S = 4.3\)
  • Profit: \(\pi = 57 \times 4.3 - 5 - 14(4.3) - 5(4.3)^2 = 87.45 > 0\)

Positive profits are possible in SR equilibrium!

Interactive: Individual and Market Supply and Demand

Factors Affecting Short-Run Equilibrium

Supply side: input prices (\(w\), \(r\)), technology, number of firms \(N_F\).

Demand side: consumer income, preferences, prices of related goods, number of consumers.

Comparative statics example:

  • \(\uparrow w\) → supply shifts left → \(\uparrow p^*\), \(\downarrow Q^*\)
  • \(\uparrow\) income (normal good) → demand shifts right → \(\uparrow p^*\), \(\uparrow Q^*\)

Long-Run Equilibrium — Zero Profit and Free Entry/Exit

A perfectly competitive industry is in long-run equilibrium if no firm has an incentive to enter or exit.

  • If \(\pi > 0\): new firms enter → supply ↑ → \(p\) ↓ → \(\pi\) ↓ toward zero
  • If \(\pi < 0\): firms exit → supply ↓ → \(p\) ↑ → \(\pi\) ↑ toward zero

Stable equilibrium: \(\pi = 0 \;\Rightarrow\; p = AC\).

Combined with \(p = MC\): the LR equilibrium price is \(p_{LR} = \min AC\).

The number of firms \(N_F\) adjusts endogenously.

Long-Run Equilibrium — Worked Example

\(Q^d = 100 - p\); all firms have \(c(q) = 20 + 5q^2\).

Find LR equilibrium price: \(\min_q AC = \min_q \left(\frac{20}{q} + 5q\right)\).

  • FOC: \(-\frac{20}{q^2} + 5 = 0 \;\Rightarrow\; q = 2\)
  • \(p^* = \min AC = \frac{20}{2} + 5 \times 2 = 20\)
  • Each firm produces \(q^S = 2\)
  • Total quantity: \(Q^d = 100 - 20 = 80\)
  • Number of firms: \(N_F = \frac{Q^d}{q^S} = \frac{80}{2} = 40\)

Summary

Week 9: Key Takeaways

  1. Short-run cost function: With capital fixed at \(\bar{K}\), cost splits into \(FC + VC(q)\)

  2. \(MC = w / MP_L\) connects production theory to cost theory

  3. MC intersects AC and AVC at their minima; U-shaped AC linked to returns to scale

  4. SR shut-down: operate if \(p \geq \min AVC\); SR supply = MC above \(\min AVC\)

  5. SR equilibrium: \(Q^d = Q^s\); firms may earn positive or negative profit

  6. LR equilibrium: free entry/exit drives \(\pi \to 0\); \(p = \min AC\); \(N\) adjusts endogenously
