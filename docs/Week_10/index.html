The Edgeworth Box

Setup: Pure Exchange Economy

Two consumers, A and B, have endowments and preferences over two goods. There is no production – the total amount of each good is fixed.

Efficiency and the Contract Curve

Pareto Efficiency

Definition: Pareto Efficiency

An allocation is Pareto efficient if there is no other feasible allocation that makes at least one consumer strictly better off without making anyone worse off.

Condition for Pareto Efficiency

At an interior Pareto efficient allocation:

\[MRS^A = MRS^B\]

Competitive Equilibrium in Exchange

Trading at a Price Ratio

Given a price ratio \(p = p_1/p_2\), each consumer faces a budget line through their endowment with slope \(-p\).

Both consumers face the same budget line – this is the key feature of competitive markets.

Each consumer then maximizes utility subject to this budget constraint, yielding their gross demands.

Interactive: Trading at a Price Ratio

Interactive: Gross Demands

For a given price ratio, each consumer chooses their optimal bundle along the budget line. These optimal bundles are their gross demands.

Interactive: Trade and MRS

The range of mutually beneficial price ratios lies between the two consumers’ marginal rates of substitution at the endowment.

Walrasian Equilibrium

Definition: Walrasian (Competitive) Equilibrium

A Walrasian equilibrium consists of a price vector \(p^*\) and an allocation \((x^{A*}, x^{B*})\) such that:

  1. Each consumer maximizes utility given prices: \(x^{i*}\) solves \(\max u^i(x)\) s.t. \(p^* \cdot x \leq p^* \cdot e^i\)
  2. Markets clear: \(x^{A*} + x^{B*} = e^A + e^B\)

First Welfare Theorem

Every Walrasian equilibrium allocation is Pareto efficient.

Offer Curves

Offer Curves

Definition: Offer Curve

An agent’s offer curve (or price-consumption curve) traces out their optimal bundle as the price ratio varies, holding the endowment fixed.

  • The offer curve passes through the endowment point (at the price ratio equal to MRS at the endowment)
  • The intersection of two agents’ offer curves gives the equilibrium

General Equilibrium with Production

GE with Production: Overview

When we add production to the exchange economy, we need:

  1. Firms that choose production plans to maximize profit
  2. Consumers who own endowments and shares in firms
  3. Market clearing in all goods and factor markets

The consumer’s income now includes:

\[m^i = p \cdot e^i + \sum_j \theta^i_j \pi_j\]

where \(\theta^i_j\) is consumer \(i\)’s ownership share in firm \(j\), and \(\pi_j\) is firm \(j\)’s profit.

Interactive: Firm Optimization on the PPF

The firm chooses the production plan \(Y^*\) on the PPF that maximizes profit. The resulting revenue becomes consumer income.

Interactive: Supply and Demand with Production

Firms maximize profits (left panel); consumers maximize utility given the resulting income (right panel). Equilibrium requires \(Y^* = X^*\).

Practice Problems

Practice 1: Two Consumers and One Producer

  • Consumer 1: \(u(x,y) = x^{0.5}y^{0.5}\), owns 50 units of good \(x\)
  • Consumer 2: \(u(x,y) = x^{0.25}y^{0.75}\), owns 40 units of good \(x\)
  • The firm is competitive, production technology \(y = \ln x\)
  • Both consumers are equal owners of the firm

Find the competitive equilibrium: prices, demands, supply, and profits.

Practice 2: Many Consumers and Many Firms

The same types of agents as before:

  • 10 consumers of type 1
  • 6 consumers of type 2
  • 8 identical firms

All consumers have equal shares in each firm, so each consumer still owns half of the representative firm.

Question: How does scaling the number of agents affect the equilibrium prices and per-capita allocations?

Practice 3: Two Consumers, One Firm, and Labor Market

  • Goods: consumption \(x\) and leisure \(l\)
  • Each consumer has 24 hours to split between leisure \(l\) and labor \(L\)
  • Consumer 1: \(u(x,l) = \min(x,l)\)
  • Consumer 2: \(u(x,l) = \min(2x,l)\)
  • The firm is competitive with technology \(x = L^{0.75}\)
  • Consumer 1 owns the firm

Find the competitive equilibrium with wages, prices, labor supply, and consumption.

Practice 4: Two Consumers and Two Firms

  • Consumer 1: \(u(x,y) = x^{0.5}y^{0.5}\), owns 50 units of capital \(K\)
  • Consumer 2: \(u(x,y) = x^{0.5}y^{0.5}\), owns 30 units of good \(x\)
  • Firm 1: technology \(x = K^{0.5}\); owned by Consumer 1
  • Firm 2: technology \(y = 2\sqrt{x}\); owned by Consumer 2

Find the competitive equilibrium: prices of \(x\), \(y\), and \(K\); production plans; demands; and profits.

Practice 5: Two Consumers, Two Firms, and Labor Market

  • Consumer 1: \(u(x,l) = x^{0.5}l^{0.5}\), owns 30 units of capital \(K\)
  • Consumer 2: \(u(x,l) = \min(x,2l)\), owns 20 units of capital \(K\)
  • Both consumers have 24 hours for leisure and labor
  • Firm 1: technology \(x = K^{1/3}L^{1/3}\); owned by Consumer 1
  • Firm 2: technology \(x = K^{2/3}\); owned by Consumer 2

Find the competitive equilibrium with all prices, factor demands, production, consumption, and profits.

Summary

Week 10: Key Takeaways

  1. Exchange economies can be analyzed in the Edgeworth box, where all feasible allocations of two goods between two consumers are represented

  2. Pareto efficiency requires \(MRS^A = MRS^B\); the set of all such points is the contract curve

  3. Walrasian equilibrium requires utility maximization by all consumers and market clearing; the First Welfare Theorem guarantees it is Pareto efficient

  4. Offer curves trace optimal demands as prices vary; their intersection determines equilibrium

  5. GE with production adds profit-maximizing firms; consumer income includes endowment value plus profit shares
