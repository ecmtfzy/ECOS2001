Part 1: Review of Exchange Economies

The Edgeworth Box (Review)

Recall: Pure Exchange Economy

Two consumers, A and B, with endowments \(\omega^A\) and \(\omega^B\) over two goods. No production – total supply is fixed.

The Edgeworth box represents all feasible allocations. A’s origin is at the bottom-left; B’s at the top-right. Any point in the box is a complete allocation of both goods.

Allocations in the Edgeworth Box (Review)

Preferences and Pareto Efficiency (Review)

An allocation is Pareto efficient if no feasible reallocation can make one consumer better off without making the other worse off.

At an interior Pareto efficient allocation, the indifference curves are tangent:

\[MRS^A = MRS^B\]

The contract curve is the set of all such Pareto efficient allocations.

The Contract Curve (Review)

Drag the allocation point to see how the indifference curves relate. The contract curve connects all points where A’s and B’s indifference curves are tangent.

Competitive Equilibrium in Exchange (Review)

Given prices \((p_1, p_2)\), each consumer faces a budget line through their endowment:

\[p_1 x_1^i + p_2 x_2^i = p_1 \omega_1^i + p_2 \omega_2^i\]

Each consumer maximizes utility subject to this constraint, yielding gross demands. A Walrasian equilibrium is a price vector \(p^*\) where markets clear: total demand = total supply.

Gross Demands in the Edgeworth Box (Review)

Welfare Theorems and Walras’ Law (Review)

First Welfare Theorem

Every Walrasian equilibrium allocation is Pareto efficient.

Second Welfare Theorem

Any Pareto efficient allocation can be achieved as a competitive equilibrium, given appropriate redistribution of initial endowments.

Walras’ Law

For any prices \((p_1, p_2)\), the total value of excess demands across all markets is zero:

\[p_1 \underbrace{(x_1^{A*} + x_1^{B*} - \omega_1^A - \omega_1^B)}_{\text{excess demand for good 1}} + p_2 \underbrace{(x_2^{A*} + x_2^{B*} - \omega_2^A - \omega_2^B)}_{\text{excess demand for good 2}} = 0\]

Implication: If one market clears, the other must clear too.

Part 2: From Exchange to Production Economies

What Changes with Production?

In Week 5 and Part 1, we studied pure exchange: consumers trade endowments, and total supply is fixed. But all five practice problems in this lecture involve firms and production.

Pure Exchange With Production
Supply Fixed (endowments) Endowments + firm output
Income Endowment value only Endowment value + profit shares
Agents Consumers only Consumers + firms
Markets Goods only Goods + factors (labor, capital)

What Carries Over

The core framework is the same: each agent optimizes, and we find prices that clear all markets. Walras’ Law, the welfare theorems, and the solution method (normalize → optimize → market clearing) all still apply.

Part 3: General Equilibrium with Production

Adding Firms to the Economy

The key extension: firms transform inputs into outputs, and consumers own shares in firms.

  1. Total supply increases: goods come from endowments and firm output
  2. Consumer income increases: \(m^i\) now includes profit shares \(\theta^i_j \pi_j\)
  3. More markets to clear: factor markets (labor, capital) in addition to goods

The consumer’s income now includes profit shares:

\[m^i = p \cdot e^i + \sum_j \theta^i_j \pi_j\]

where \(\theta^i_j\) is consumer \(i\)’s ownership share in firm \(j\), and \(\pi_j\) is firm \(j\)’s profit.

The Production Possibilities Frontier (PPF)

Definition: PPF

The production possibilities frontier is the set of all maximum output combinations that can be produced given the economy’s resources and technology.

With two goods \(x\) and \(y\), if the firm uses input \(z\) with technology \(y = f(z)\) and \(x = \bar{z} - z\), the PPF is:

\[y = f(\bar{z} - x)\]

The slope of the PPF is the marginal rate of transformation (MRT): the rate at which the economy must give up good \(x\) to produce one more unit of good \(y\).

Firm Optimization on the PPF

The firm chooses the production plan \(Y^*\) on the PPF that maximizes profit: the point where the iso-profit line (slope \(= -p_x/p_y\)) is tangent to the PPF, i.e., \(MRT = p_x/p_y\).

Supply and Demand with Production

Firms maximize profits (left panel); consumers maximize utility given the resulting income (right panel). Equilibrium requires \(Y^* = X^*\).

General Equilibrium with Production

Adjust prices to find the equilibrium where the firm’s optimal supply \(Y^*\) equals the consumer’s optimal demand \(X^*\).

Equilibrium as a Function of Preferences

The equilibrium price ratio depends on consumer preferences (\(\alpha\)). The point where \(Y^* = X^*\) shifts as preferences change.

Part 4: Solving General Equilibrium Problems

Solving GE: Pure Exchange

Step-by-Step Method

  1. Normalize one price: set \(p_2 = 1\), write \(p = p_1\)
  2. Budget constraints: \(p \, x_1^i + x_2^i = p \, \omega_1^i + \omega_2^i\) for each consumer \(i\)
  3. Solve each consumer’s utility maximization \(\Rightarrow\) demand functions \(x_1^i(p)\), \(x_2^i(p)\)
  4. Market clearing (one market suffices by Walras’ Law):

\[\sum_i x_1^i(p) = \sum_i \omega_1^i \quad \Rightarrow \quad \text{solve for } p^*\]

  1. Substitute \(p^*\) back to get equilibrium allocation \((x^{A*}, x^{B*})\)

Solving GE: With Production

Additional Steps for Production Economies

  1. Firm’s problem: \(\max_y \; p \cdot y\) subject to technology \(\Rightarrow\) supply \(y^*(p)\) and profit \(\pi(p)\)
  2. Consumer income includes profit shares:

\[m^i(p) = p \cdot e^i + \theta^i \, \pi(p)\]

  1. Solve consumer optimization with endogenous income \(m^i(p)\)
  2. Market clearing for goods:

\[\sum_i x_j^i(p) = \sum_i \omega_j^i + y_j^*(p)\]

Key Difference

In pure exchange, income is exogenous (just endowment value). With production, income depends on prices through profits.

Solving GE: With Labor Markets

When leisure and labor are goods:

  • Consumer \(i\)’s endowment includes time (e.g., \(T = 24\) hours)
  • Budget: \(p \cdot x + w \cdot l \leq w \cdot T + \text{profit income}\)
  • Labor supply: \(L^i = T - l^{i*}(p, w)\)
  • Firm’s labor demand comes from profit maximization: \(\max_{L} \; p \cdot f(K, L) - w \cdot L\)
  • Market clearing: \(\sum_i L^i(p,w) = \sum_j L_j^d(p,w)\)

Normalize one price (e.g., \(p = 1\)) and solve for the wage \(w^*\).

The Social Planner’s Problem

The social planner finds all Pareto efficient allocations by solving:

\[\max_{x_1, x_2, \ldots} \; u^1(x_1) \quad \text{s.t.} \quad u^i(x_i) \geq \bar{u}_i \;\;\forall\, i \geq 2, \quad \text{resource constraints}\]

Connection to Welfare Theorems

  • First Welfare Theorem: the competitive equilibrium is one point on the Pareto set
  • Second Welfare Theorem: every point on the Pareto set can be achieved as a competitive equilibrium (with appropriate endowment redistribution)
  • Varying \(\bar{u}_2, \bar{u}_3, \ldots\) traces out the entire Pareto set (or equivalently, the Utility Possibilities Frontier)

The Utility Possibilities Frontier

The UPF shows all achievable \((u^A, u^B)\) pairs from Pareto efficient allocations. Drag the allocation in the Edgeworth box and toggle the UPF.

Example: Two Consumers and One Producer

  • Consumer 1: \(u = x^{0.5}y^{0.5}\), owns 50 units of \(x\). Consumer 2: \(u = x^{0.25}y^{0.75}\), owns 40 units of \(x\)
  • Firm: \(y = \ln x_f\) (uses \(x\) as input, produces \(y\)). Both consumers own 50%

Step 1 – Firm’s problem. Normalize \(p_y = 1\), let \(p = p_x\).

\[\max_{x_f} \; \ln x_f - p \cdot x_f \quad \Rightarrow \quad x_f^* = \frac{1}{p}, \quad y_f^* = \ln\tfrac{1}{p}, \quad \pi^* = \ln\tfrac{1}{p} - 1\]

Step 2 – Consumer demands. Each consumer’s income includes profit shares:

  • \(m_1 = 50p + \tfrac{1}{2}\pi^*\), demands: \(x_1 = \tfrac{m_1}{2p}\), \(y_1 = \tfrac{m_1}{2}\)
  • \(m_2 = 40p + \tfrac{1}{2}\pi^*\), demands: \(x_2 = \tfrac{m_2}{4p}\), \(y_2 = \tfrac{3m_2}{4}\)

Example: Equilibrium

Step 3 – Market clearing for good \(x\): \(\;x_1 + x_2 + x_f = 90\)

\[\frac{m_1}{2p} + \frac{m_2}{4p} + \frac{1}{p} = 90\]

Substituting \(m_1\), \(m_2\), and \(\pi^*\), this simplifies to:

\[-3\ln p + 5 = 440p\]

This is a transcendental equation — solve numerically: \(p^* \approx 0.034\).

Firm Consumer 1 Consumer 2
Good \(x\) \(x_f \approx 29.2\) (input) \(x_1 \approx 42.3\) \(x_2 \approx 18.6\)
Good \(y\) \(y_f \approx 3.37\) (output) \(y_1 \approx 1.45\) \(y_2 \approx 1.92\)
Profit/Income \(\pi \approx 2.37\) \(m_1 \approx 2.90\) \(m_2 \approx 2.56\)

Example: Social Planner’s Problem

The social planner characterizes all Pareto efficient allocations:

\[\max \; x_1^{0.5} y_1^{0.5}\]

\[\text{s.t.} \quad x_2^{0.25} y_2^{0.75} = \bar{u}_2, \qquad y_f = \ln x_f\]

\[x_1 + x_2 + x_f = 90, \qquad y_1 + y_2 = y_f\]

Varying \(\bar{u}_2\) traces out the Pareto set. The competitive equilibrium is one point on this set (First Welfare Theorem).

Part 5: Practice Problems

Practice 1: Many Consumers and Many Firms

The same types of agents as in the example above:

  • 10 consumers of type 1
  • 6 consumers of type 2
  • 8 identical firms

Question: How does scaling the number of agents affect the equilibrium prices and per-capita allocations?

Practice 1: Solution

Each consumer’s individual problem is the same as in the example (same utility, endowment, and profit share \(\pi/2\)). But the aggregation changes because the ratio of consumer types shifts from 1:1 to 10:6.

Market clearing for good \(x\): \(\;10x_1 + 6x_2 + 8x_f = 740\)

\[10\!\left(25 + \frac{\pi}{4p}\right) + 6\!\left(10 + \frac{\pi}{8p}\right) + \frac{8}{p} = 740\]

This simplifies to: \(\;-3.25\ln p + 4.75 = 430p\)

Compare with the example: \(\;-3\ln p + 5 = 440p\). The price ratio \(p^*\) is different because the economy has relatively more type-1 consumers (who spend a larger share on \(x\)).

Practice 1: Social Planner

\[\max \; x_1^{0.5} y_1^{0.5}\]

\[\text{s.t.} \quad x_i^{0.5} y_i^{0.5} = \bar{u}_i \; (i = 2,\ldots,10), \qquad x_i^{0.25} y_i^{0.75} = \bar{u}_i \; (i = 11,\ldots,16)\]

\[y_f = \textstyle\sum_{j=1}^{8} \ln x_f^j, \qquad \sum_{i=1}^{16} x_i + \sum_{j=1}^{8} x_f^j = 740, \qquad \sum_{i=1}^{16} y_i = y_f\]

Practice 2: Two Consumers, One Firm, and Labor Market

  • Goods: consumption \(x\) and leisure \(l\)
  • Each consumer has 24 hours to split between leisure \(l\) and labor \(L\)
  • Consumer 1: \(u(x,l) = \min(x,l)\)
  • Consumer 2: \(u(x,l) = \min(2x,l)\)
  • The firm is competitive with technology \(x = L^{0.75}\)
  • Consumer 1 owns the firm

Find the competitive equilibrium with wages, prices, labor supply, and consumption.

Practice 2: Solution

Normalize \(p_x = 1\), let \(w\) = wage.

Firm: \(\max_L \; L^{0.75} - wL\) \(\;\Rightarrow\;\) \(L^d = (0.75/w)^4\), \(\;x_f = (0.75/w)^3\), \(\;\pi = 0.25 \cdot (0.75/w)^3\)

Consumer 1 (Leontief \(\min(x,l)\)): \(x_1 = l_1 = \frac{24w + \pi}{1 + w}\), \(\;L_1 = 24 - l_1\)

Consumer 2 (Leontief \(\min(2x,l)\)): \(x_2 = \frac{24w}{1 + 2w}\), \(\;l_2 = 2x_2\), \(\;L_2 = 24 - l_2\)

Market clearing (labor): \(L_1 + L_2 = L^d\) \(\;\Rightarrow\;\) solve numerically for \(w^*\).

Practice 2: Social Planner

\[\max \; \min(x_1, l_1)\]

\[\text{s.t.} \quad \min(2x_2, l_2) = \bar{u}_2, \qquad x_f = L^{0.75}\]

\[x_1 + x_2 = x_f, \qquad L = 48 - l_1 - l_2, \qquad 0 \leq l_1, l_2 \leq 24\]

Practice 3: Two Consumers and Two Firms

  • Consumer 1: \(u(x,y) = x^{0.5}y^{0.5}\), owns 50 units of capital \(K\)
  • Consumer 2: \(u(x,y) = x^{0.5}y^{0.5}\), owns 30 units of good \(x\)
  • Firm 1: technology \(x = K^{0.5}\); owned by Consumer 1
  • Firm 2: technology \(y = 2\sqrt{x}\); owned by Consumer 2

Find the competitive equilibrium: prices of \(x\), \(y\), and \(K\); production plans; demands; and profits.

Practice 3: Solution

Normalize \(p_y = 1\), let \(p = p_x\), \(r = p_K\).

Firm 1: \(\max_K \; p \cdot K^{0.5} - rK\) \(\;\Rightarrow\;\) \(K^d = (p/2r)^2\), \(\;x_1^s = p/(2r)\), \(\;\pi_1 = p^2/(4r)\)

Firm 2: \(\max_x \; 2\sqrt{x} - px\) \(\;\Rightarrow\;\) \(x^d = 1/p^2\), \(\;y^s = 2/p\), \(\;\pi_2 = 1/p\)

Incomes: \(m_1 = 50r + \pi_1\), \(\;m_2 = 30p + \pi_2\)

Demands (both Cobb-Douglas \(\alpha = 0.5\)):

  • \(x_1 = m_1/(2p)\), \(y_1 = m_1/2\); \(\;x_2 = m_2/(2p)\), \(y_2 = m_2/2\)

Market clearing for \(K\), \(x\), \(y\) (Walras’ Law: only need 2 of 3) \(\;\Rightarrow\;\) solve for \(p^*\), \(r^*\).

Practice 3: Social Planner

\[\max \; \sqrt{x_1 y_1}\]

\[\text{s.t.} \quad \sqrt{x_2 y_2} = \bar{u}_2, \qquad x_{f1} = K^{0.5}, \qquad y_{f2} = 2x_{f2}^{0.5}\]

\[x_1 + x_2 + x_{f2} = x_{f1} + 30, \qquad y_1 + y_2 = y_{f2}, \qquad K = 50\]

Practice 4: Two Consumers, Two Firms, and Labor Market

  • Consumer 1: \(u(x,l) = x^{0.5}l^{0.5}\), owns 30 units of capital \(K\)
  • Consumer 2: \(u(x,l) = \min(x,2l)\), owns 20 units of capital \(K\)
  • Both consumers have 24 hours for leisure and labor
  • Firm 1: technology \(x = K^{1/3}L^{1/3}\); owned by Consumer 1
  • Firm 2: technology \(x = K^{2/3}\); owned by Consumer 2

Find the competitive equilibrium with all prices, factor demands, production, consumption, and profits.

Practice 4: Solution

Normalize \(p_x = 1\), let \(w\) = wage, \(r = p_K\).

Firm 1: \(\max_{K,L} \; K^{1/3}L^{1/3} - rK - wL\). FOCs give \(L_1/K_1 = r/w\).

\(\Rightarrow\) \(K_1 = \frac{1}{27wr^2}\), \(\;L_1 = \frac{1}{27w^2r}\), \(\;x_{f1} = \frac{1}{9wr}\), \(\;\pi_1 = \frac{1}{27wr}\)

Firm 2: \(\max_K \; K^{2/3} - rK\) \(\;\Rightarrow\;\) \(K_2 = \frac{8}{27r^3}\), \(\;x_{f2} = \frac{4}{9r^2}\), \(\;\pi_2 = \frac{4}{27r^2}\)

Consumer 1 (CD \(\alpha = 0.5\)): \(m_1 = 30r + 24w + \pi_1\), \(\;x_1 = m_1/2\), \(\;l_1 = m_1/(2w)\)

Consumer 2 (Leontief \(\min(x,2l)\)): \(m_2 = 20r + 24w + \pi_2\), \(\;x_2 = 2l_2\), \(\;l_2 = m_2/(2 + w)\)

Market clearing: \(K_1 + K_2 = 50\), labor, goods (Walras’ Law: need 2 of 3) \(\;\Rightarrow\;\) solve for \(w^*\), \(r^*\).

Practice 4: Social Planner

\[\max \; \sqrt{x_1 l_1}\]

\[\text{s.t.} \quad \min(x_2, 2l_2) = \bar{u}_2, \qquad x_{f1} = K_1^{1/3}L_1^{1/3}, \qquad x_{f2} = K_2^{2/3}\]

\[x_1 + x_2 = x_{f1} + x_{f2}, \qquad L_1 = 48 - l_1 - l_2\]

\[K_1 + K_2 = 50, \qquad 0 \leq l_1, l_2 \leq 24\]

Summary

Week 10: Key Takeaways

  1. Review: The Edgeworth box, Pareto efficiency (\(MRS^A = MRS^B\)), contract curve, Walrasian equilibrium, and welfare theorems were introduced in Week 5

  2. From exchange to production: the core framework (optimize → market clearing) carries over; what changes is income (adds profit shares) and supply (adds firm output)

  3. GE with production: consumer income includes endowment value plus profit shares: \(m^i = p \cdot e^i + \theta^i \pi\)

  4. Solving GE: normalize a price, derive demands, apply market clearing, solve for equilibrium prices

  5. Walras’ Law means you only need to clear \(n-1\) of \(n\) markets

  6. Social planner’s problem characterizes the Pareto set; the competitive equilibrium is one point on it (First Welfare Theorem), and every point can be decentralized (Second Welfare Theorem)
